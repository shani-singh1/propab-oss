Use structured divergent-thinking moves to escape the obvious next-parameter hypothesis
and reach genuinely new directions. These techniques expand the space of ideas; they do
NOT lower the bar — every idea you keep must be forged into a falsifiable, scoped,
adversarially-testable hypothesis (that is non-negotiable in this engine).

**Divergent moves (to generate candidate directions):**
- **Cross-domain analogy** — import a mechanism, invariant, or method from a different
  field and ask whether an analogue governs this problem.
- **Assumption reversal** — take a load-bearing assumption in the current best approach and
  ask what follows if the opposite holds.
- **Scale / regime shift** — push the question to a different size, dimension, density, or
  limit; effects that vanish or emerge at a new scale are often where the frontier is.
- **Constraint manipulation** — add, remove, or tighten a constraint (symmetry, resource,
  structure) and ask what new construction or bound becomes reachable.
- **Mechanism substitution** — propose a different underlying mechanism for a known effect
  and design the experiment that would distinguish them.

**Convergent filter (mandatory — apply to every candidate before you keep it):**
1. Can it be REFUTED by a concrete observation/computation? If not, discard it.
2. Is it NOVEL — not a known/tabulated value, not a re-derivation, not a parameter sweep of
   a settled result? If it only re-measures the known, discard it.
3. Can you name the adversarial test (null, hold-out, or witness) that decides it, and the
   scope (population/distribution/failure mode) it lives in? If not, sharpen it until you can.

Aim for candidates that are conceptually DISTINCT from each other (different mechanisms /
invariants / regimes), not several dressings of one idea. Creativity is for widening the
search; the falsifiability filter is what turns a brainstormed possibility into a
Propab hypothesis worth running.
